This page is for defense and training only. It does not provide instructions to break into systems. The goal is to help people spot suspicious behavior early, follow policy, and report issues promptly.

Understanding attacker patterns helps us improve controls, training, and incident response. We focus on what defenders can observe and the good habits that stop issues before they start.

The typical attack lifecycle (high level)

1) Recon & Preparation

Information gathering

Public info, impersonation attempts, or probing for weak process points.

2) Initial Access

Social or technical

3) Persistence & Movement

Staying in

Abusing legitimate tools, reusing access, or escalating privileges.

4) Actions on Objectives

Impact

Data access, fraud, disruption, or extortion; sometimes slow and subtle.

Common technique families (non-exhaustive)

Social engineering

Manipulating people with urgency, authority, or familiarity. Examples include fake approvals, spoofed ‚ÄúIT support,‚Äù or gift-card scams.

Defender tipsVerify unusual requests via known channels, use approved ticketing, and report look-alike domains or unexpected login prompts.

Credential misuse

Attackers rely on weak, reused, or phished credentials‚Äîoften combined with notification fatigue.

Defender tipsRequire MFA, encourage password managers, monitor for impossible travel, and watch for multiple login failures across accounts.

Exploiting outdated software

Unpatched systems and shadow IT widen the attack surface.

Defender tipsMaintain clear patch cycles, inventory systems, and retire or isolate unsupported tools.

Abusing legitimate tools (‚Äúliving off the land‚Äù)

Using built-in admin utilities or common services to blend in with normal activity.

Defender tipsApply least-privilege, log administrative actions, and baseline normal behavior to spot anomalies.

Data exfiltration & extortion

Moving data out via email, cloud sync, or encrypted channels; threats to leak data to pressure payment.

Defender tipsUse DLP where appropriate, monitor unusual transfers, and classify sensitive data to enforce stricter controls.

Good habits that stop incidents

  1. Use MFA everywhere possible and approve only prompts you initiated.
  2. Verify unusual requests with a phone call or chat using known contact info.
  3. Keep devices and apps updated on a regular cadence.
  4. Use approved tools & storage for work data; avoid personal accounts.
  5. Report quickly‚Äîeven if unsure. Early notice limits impact.
