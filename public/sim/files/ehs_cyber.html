TL;DR: Safety is holistic. Physical hazards (chemicals, machinery, slips) and digital hazards (phishing, ransomware, data loss) share the same controls: identify, assess, control, and report.

Why EHS and Cybersecurity are linked

Modern operations run on technology. A malicious email can stop a production line; a mishandled chemical SDS can leak customer data. Both disciplines protect people, product, and data—and both use layered defenses.

People

Training, PPE, phishing awareness, and reporting culture reduce incidents.

Process

Permits-to-work ↔ access control, change management, and approvals.

Technology

Ventilation, guards, and sensors ↔ MFA, patching, backups, and monitoring.

Shared Controls, Different Hazards

Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) ↔ Account Lockout

  • EHS: Isolate energy before servicing.
  • Cyber: Disable accounts/ssh keys before offboarding.
  • Both: Verify isolation before work begins.

Chemical Hygiene ↔ Data Hygiene

  • EHS: Label containers, keep SDS, proper storage.
  • Cyber: Classify data, label sensitivity, store securely.
  • Both: Secondary containment: safes or encryption.

Cleanrooms, Labs, and the "FAB"

Removable Media

No personal USBs. Use IT-issued encrypted drives only. Treat unknown USBs like unknown chemicals—assume hazardous.

Phishing ↔ Process Upsets

Unexpected email instructions can cause material scrap just like a wrong valve position. Stop, verify, then act.

Visitor & Badge Control

Badge visibly. Challenge tailgaters. Escort visitors; restrict photography and network access.

Waste & Asset Disposal

Disposal routes protect both the environment and our data.

  • E‑waste: Return laptops/phones to IT for certified wipe & destruction.
  • Paper: Place sensitive printouts in locked shred bins—never mixed recycling.
  • Chemicals: Follow waste labels and satellite accumulation rules; keep lids closed.

Incident Response: One Playbook

Report Early

Spills, near misses, suspicious emails, lost badges, or malware pop‑ups → report within 15 min via Safety & Security in the Helpdesk.

Stabilize

Contain safely: cordon area, disconnect from network if instructed, preserve evidence, document who/what/when.

OT/ICS Considerations

  • Patch windows and changeovers are scheduled with Production and IT.
  • Never plug laptops into control networks without approval and updated AV.
  • Backups are treated like safety interlocks—tested monthly.

Daily Safety × Security Checklist

Start of Shift

  • PPE on; badge visible
  • Login with MFA
  • Check spill kits & backups status board

During Shift

  • No drinks near keyboards/panels
  • Verify email senders on change orders
  • Secure ports/cabinets between tasks

End of Shift

  • Clean desk: lock papers & devices
  • Log out / lock screen
  • Dispose of samples & prints correctly

Myth vs Fact

Myth

“Ransomware only affects office PCs.”

Fact

Plants and labs rely on PCs for recipes, batch records, and environmental controls. Downtime is a safety risk.

1‑Minute Micro‑Quiz

Test yourself—no grades, just learning.

  1. You find a labeled beaker and an unlabeled thumb drive. Which do you report first?
  2. A vendor emails a new valve setting for today’s run. What’s your verification step?
  3. What should backups be treated like: PPE, signage, or housekeeping?
